Key Monetary Policy Episodes, 1979-2023
1979
ContractionaryThe Volcker Disinflation
Fed Chair Paul Volcker raised the federal funds rate above 20 percent to eliminate entrenched double-digit inflation expectations. The resulting contraction drove unemployment to approximately 10.8 percent by late 1982. The policy succeeded in permanently re-anchoring long-run inflation expectations and restoring the credibility the Federal Reserve had lost during the preceding decade of accommodative policy.
2001
ExpansionaryPost-Dot-Com Easing Cycle
Following the collapse of technology asset valuations and the economic disruption of September 2001, the Fed reduced the federal funds rate to 1.0 percent and maintained an accommodative stance through 2004. Subsequent analysis, including Taylor (2009), identified this period as one in which the actual rate fell materially below Taylor Rule prescriptions, contributing to the credit expansion that preceded the 2008 financial crisis.
2008
Zero Lower BoundFinancial Crisis and Quantitative Easing
The Global Financial Crisis drove the FOMC to cut the federal funds rate to the 0 to 0.25 percent range in December 2008, reaching the zero lower bound for the first time in the modern era. With conventional rate policy exhausted, the Fed introduced Quantitative Easing, purchasing mortgage-backed securities and Treasury securities to compress longer-term yields and provide additional monetary accommodation through balance sheet expansion.
2022
ContractionaryPost-COVID Tightening Cycle
CPI inflation reached 9.1 percent in June 2022, driven by pandemic-era supply-chain disruptions and fiscal expansion. The Federal Reserve executed 525 basis points of cumulative rate increases across eleven consecutive FOMC meetings, the most aggressive tightening sequence in four decades. Disinflation was achieved without a recession, a substantially better outcome than the Volcker episode, attributable in part to the credibility the institution had accumulated over the intervening period.
